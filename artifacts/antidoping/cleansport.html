About CleanSport

We are the independent organization dedicated to protecting the rights of clean athletes and preserving the integrity of sport worldwide.

Our Mission

To lead a collaborative worldwide movement for doping-free sport. We believe that athletes have a fundamental right to participate in sport free from the use of performance-enhancing drugs.

Through education, scientific research, intelligent testing, and transparent results management, we aim to eliminate doping from competition and ensure a level playing field for all.

Our Values

The principles that guide our decisions, our operations, and our commitment to the athletic community.

Integrity

We operate with absolute transparency, honesty, and ethical conduct in all our operations and adjudications.

Excellence

We employ world-class scientific research, cutting-edge intelligence, and rigorous testing protocols.

Respect

We respect the rights of athletes, honoring their dedication to their sport and their commitment to fair play.

Strict Liability

The principle of strict liability means that an athlete is ultimately responsible for any prohibited substance found in their body, regardless of how it got there.

"I didn't know" is not a valid defense.

The Testing Process

Transparent, rigorous, and respectful. Understand what happens during a doping control session and your responsibilities.

1. Whereabouts & Notification

Elite athletes in a Registered Testing Pool (RTP) must provide accurate "Whereabouts" information, including a 60-minute time slot every day where they are available for testing.

A Doping Control Officer (DCO) can notify you for a test anytime, anywhere. Upon notification, they will show identification, explain your rights, and you must stay in direct sight of the DCO until the process is complete.

Your Rights

  • Have a representative and/or interpreter present
  • Ask for additional information about the process
  • Request modifications if you are a minor or have an impairment
  • Document any concerns on the Doping Control Form

2. Sample Collection

Collection Types

  • Urine: You must provide a minimum of 90mL under direct observation by a DCO of the same gender.
  • Blood: Collected by a qualified Blood Collection Officer (BCO). Usually less than 2 tablespoons.
  • Dried Blood Spot (DBS): A newer, less invasive method collecting a few drops of blood on a card.

You will choose a sealed sample collection kit. You are responsible for ensuring the kit is intact and handling your own sample until it is sealed.

The sample is divided into an 'A' and 'B' bottle. Both are securely sealed. You will verify that the code numbers on the bottles match the forms.

3. Paperwork & Analysis

You will declare any medications or supplements taken in the last 7 days on the Doping Control Form. You will then sign the form, confirming the procedure was conducted properly.

The samples are sent anonymously (only the code numbers are visible) to a WADA-accredited laboratory. The 'A' sample is analyzed, and the 'B' sample is securely stored.

Results Management

If the 'A' sample returns an Adverse Analytical Finding (AAF), you will be notified. You have the right to request the analysis of the 'B' sample.

Therapeutic Use Exemptions (TUE)

Athletes may have illnesses or conditions that require them to take medications. If the medication an athlete is required to take falls under the Prohibited List, a Therapeutic Use Exemption (TUE) may give that athlete the authorization to take the needed medicine.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is strict liability?

The principle that athletes are responsible for any prohibited substance found in their body, regardless of intent.

Can I refuse a doping test?

Refusing or failing to submit to sample collection without compelling justification is an Anti-Doping Rule Violation (ADRV).

Are nutritional supplements safe?

No supplement is 100% safe. They carry a high risk of contamination with prohibited substances.

How long is a TUE valid?

TUEs have a specific duration indicated on the certificate, usually between 1 to 4 years depending on the condition.
